During the second quarter of the equity market regained much of the ground at loss in February and March, driven by unprecedented government stimulus and an improving economic outlook. Consumer discretionary, information technology, and energy led the market for the quarter. Valuations spreads, the gap between the valuations of the most and least expensive stocks, widened in February and March, as they usually have involved markets. Stocks rallied this quarter, but valuations spread stayed high, suggesting that investors remain worried. When valuations spreads have been at comparable levels in the past, the market has fared well over the following 12 months, with an average return of 18%. The personal savings rate rose in March, and, despite the 33% in April, the highest level on record. The combination of government stimulus, which put cash directly into consumers' pockets and lockdowns, which prevented them from spending it, contributed to the dramatic jump. A stockpile of savings may give consumers greater spending power than they've had in past recessions. Historically, high savings rates have fueled strong consumption growth. After periods in which the personal savings rate has been in the top quartile of its historic range, consumption growth has averaged about 4% per year over the following three years. For context, consumption grew just under 3% and the three years after savings rates were in the bottom quartile of their historical range.
