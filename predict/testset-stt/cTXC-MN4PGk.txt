Hello everybody. If the monthly quarterly and annual assessments of the financial markets were a school report card, it would be tempting to write could do better as we head into the summer period. With global equities down slightly on year to date and emerging market shares bearing far worse, performances do not seem to reflect the solid global economy, or the healthy macroeconomic fundamentals that bowed wealth of business. The resurgence in volatility in June, while less pronounced than in February, reflects investor indecision over which scenario is most likely. This reluctance to take a stand has resulted in directionally strengthened for bond yields and G3 exchange rates, but in a divergence of performance for the major equity indices. With 2020 hindsight, it's easy to see that volatility has been driven by political and geopolitical uncertainties, which came to the fore in June with new and increasingly open trade skirmishes between the United States and its main partners. The Trump administration believes that China is dragging its feet in intellectual property negotiations and announced a 25% tariff on more than 800 Chinese products, effective July 6th. The US President also raised the possibility of targeting imports from Europe, auto imports in particular. In the wake of these developments, investors overlooked the fact that worldwide economic figures were a little more encouraging than in May, and that central banks are sticking to their dovish line. Key rates are still being normalized and the winding down of ultra-accommodative policies is underway, but at a very slow pace.
