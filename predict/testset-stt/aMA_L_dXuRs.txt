An acute liquidity crunch exacerbated the volatility, causing correlations to rise across most asset categories. For the full quarter, high quality bonds and gold provided a safe haven, but almost all riskier asset classes registered double-digit declines. The global spread of the coronavirus and the clampdowns on economic activity that accompanied it created the catalyst for the market gyrations. What had been a long-running late cycle phase for much of the world turned into recession. While the progression of the virus was not uniform among countries, the shutdown of travel and other activities negatively reinforced the global nature of the downturn. The onset of US recession began in March while China's economy showed some signs of bottoming, but remained in a deep contraction. In the US, the sudden stop nature of the downturn was both unusual and dramatic. Near record low levels of unemployment reversed abruptly, and new unemployment claims surged to record highs. Industries most directly and immediately impacted, such as travel, hotels and restaurants, account for roughly one-fifth of all US employment and economic output. From an investment perspective, the negative shock to corporate profit growth is likely to be worse than anticipated. The progression of new virus infections will likely tell us something about how long the most restrictive economic measures will remain in place. The virus began in China and the flattening rate of new cases in that country prompted a gradual removal of the strictest measures during March. On the brighter side, US monetary and fiscal policy makers responded rapidly to address the deterioration in financial and economic conditions. The Federal Reserve cut its policy rate to zero and announced several new facilities designed to improve financial market liquidity. The Fed also initiated an open-ended quantitative easing campaign that added nearly $1 trillion to its balance sheet in March with the promise of much more to come. The Federal Government improved legislation to provide more than $2 trillion of support to healthcare relief efforts, households and businesses.
