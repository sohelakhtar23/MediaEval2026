The 2008-09 Great Recession forced the World Central Banks to implement unconventional monetary policies. Ten years later, they appear to be becoming more conventional. Six central banks still have negative policy rates. Central banks in many smaller economies, yet to go down the unconventional monetary policy path have been cutting rates this year and are now running short of traditional ammunition bringing unconventional monetary policy closer into view. This all begs the question, who could be next if global growth keeps slowing? In seeking which central bank could be next to go down the unconventional monetary policy path, we use a scorecard methodology based on 14 indicators for 19 countries whose central banks currently have 25 to 425 basis points of space left to cut their interest rates. We first assess potential contenders by identifying those countries that have underwhelming macroeconomic performances and are running low on traditional monetary and fiscal policy space. For example, a country with below trend growth and inflation, very low interest rates and high public debt would be a potential contender. We then gauge how suitable unconventional monetary policies might be in these 19 countries by considering the structure of their financial systems, how susceptible their banking and external sectors are to the negative side effects of unconventional monetary policies and their overall financial stability risks. Our final verdict brings the contenders and the suitability parts of our scorecard together. We single out 7 of the 19 economies that are most likely to move to unconventional monetary policies and they are in this order, Israel, US, Thailand, UK, South Korea and Australia. We also apply our methodology to the six countries already with subzero policy rates and find that the negative side effects of unconventional monetary policy are becoming apparent. An uneasy conclusion is that some countries, most notably Japan, might need more policy support, yet they have largely exhausted both their traditional policy limits and unconventional policy limits too. Finally, our scorecard results support our rate strategy convictions. We are received two year and ten year NDIS in Thailand and also received two year Korea NDIS. In Australia we are receiving February 2020 OIS and for FX we expect rising expectations of unconventional monetary policies to weaken the Korean one but it could actually strengthen the Thai Bart on the back of strong foreign bond inflows while we expect the Aussie dollar to weaken.
