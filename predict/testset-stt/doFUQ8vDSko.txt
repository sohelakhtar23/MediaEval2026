Technological change is not new and concerns about technological changes not new either. We are going through a period of profound and disruptive changes in the labour market driven by globalization and technology. These changes are impacting the relationship between the labour market and inflation, making the job of central banks much harder, much more difficult for them to achieve their inflation targets with these structural headwinds. But they are also changing and will have very big impacts on how we consume the sort of decisions that we make in our spending patterns, but also where we want to live and whether we want to rent a property or buy. It's also affecting the political arena and we've seen this effect coming through in the surprising and somewhat disruptive results of the Brexit referendum, the unexpected victory of Donald Trump in the US elections and more recently with the unexpected Italian election outcome earlier this year. One of the conclusions that we draw is that there is a strong link between disruptive changes in the labour market and what we see happening on the political front and the populism. We may not have seen the peak level of populism so far. We need to keep a very close eye on developments in the labour market if we are to manage client assets carefully taking into account possible political risk.
