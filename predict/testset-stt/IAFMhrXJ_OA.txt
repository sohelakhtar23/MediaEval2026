We've all seen the headlines. The web of life is falling apart, but this is a man-made crisis, meaning that we can and we must act. The biodiversity crisis will become a core focus of our stewardship work, including corporate engagement, proxy voting, and public policy advocacy. Climate change is a significant driver of biodiversity loss, but biodiversity loss is also driving climate change. These twin crises are inextricably linked. We cannot address one without considering the other. So when we speak with companies about climate change, we're also asking about biodiversity, and we're stressing the urgency of this crisis, because extinction is forever. We must act today. We will continue to engage with companies to halt deforestation. We'll also use our water footprint to engage companies with the greatest exposure to areas of water scarcity. We're in the midst of a very productive dialogue on the apparent link between certain pesticides and alarming declines in pollinators, and we plan to expand this work. We're also working with our portfolio managers to integrate corporate engagement into funds that express an environmental theme, or that hold companies that have particularly significant impacts on nature. For example, we're developing a corporate engagement strategy for our Blue Economy ETF, focused on protecting marine habitats. We'll continue our strong record of support for shareholder proposals on these topics, and we'll consider submitting proposals of our own, focused on the biodiversity crisis. We'll need strong public policy, so we intend to help organize an investor statement in advance of the next meeting of the Convention on Biological Diversity in the Fall. We'll also be asking corporations to lobby for nature. Most importantly, we cannot do this alone. That's why we've been working with like-minded investors to help organize a collaborative investor engagement focused on biodiversity. And we'll have more to come, because the future is literally in our hands.
