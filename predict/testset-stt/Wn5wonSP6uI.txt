Structural drivers of food demand, rising EM population and income growth, coupled with an increasing appetite for more protein, remain very strong. The supply side of the food equation is also tightening, as the eight-year trend decline in food prices have discouraged new agricultural investment at a time of decreasing arable land and water supply. There's also been an increased frequency of extreme weather events and the outbreak of the African, swine fever. Michael took us through an amour of food vulnerability index or NFVI for short. Our NFVI objectively estimates a country's exposure to large food price swings, depending on nominal GDP per capita, the share of food in household consumption and net food imports. Our ranking of 110 economies, highlight Libya, Tajikistan, Montenegro, Syria and Algeria, as the five economies most vulnerable to a sustained rise in food prices. The 50 most vulnerable countries in our NFVI are largely EM and collectively make up 26% of world GDP, but a much greater than 59% of the world's population. Frontier economies are most at risk. The top 30 vulnerable economies in our NFVI are nearly all frontier economies. Their outstanding hard currency debt has tripled over the last five years and many also owe hidden debt to China, putting them at risk of being in a debt trap. As these governments have little fiscal space, they could impose trade controls to protect their consumers in the event of higher food prices and this could amplify the food price surge globally. Our analysis of the 2010-2011 food price surge confirms that economic fundamentals deteriorated much more significantly in what the NFVI classified as the 30 most vulnerable economies than in the 30 least vulnerable ones. These countries saw weaker growth, higher inflation, a wider fiscal deficit and steeper policy rate hikes. Overall, we would argue that in the next surge in food prices, the most vulnerable countries in our NFVI are probably going to be affected more than in past episodes of food price surges. This is for two reasons. One, it's these economies that are also most vulnerable to climate change induced disruptions to agriculture. And two, it's also these economies that have experienced a sharp rise in debt to dangerously high levels. So it's really double trouble for the top 30 economies in our NFVI.
