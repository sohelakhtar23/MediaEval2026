Companies don't operate in a vacuum. They're affected by society and have impacts on society. Understanding that relationship, which is the focus of our sustainability research, has never been more important with companies' role in society under growing scrutiny. For most of the last 70 years, companies were able to operate by pushing a lot of the costs created by their products, services and operations back onto society. Effective tax rates, workers' share of GDP, regulatory compliance costs, fines, all fell for many decades, going back to at least the mid-20th century. Governments had to pick up a lot of the strain. Twice as many employed workers need welfare payments to make ends meet as was the case a decade ago. The UN's warned climate action can't wait and government budgets have been stretched to the limit. We're seeing a response and a rebalancing. Since the financial crisis, voters have given governments a stronger mandate to regulate irresponsible corporate behaviour. Companies are being forced to internalise costs they previously pushed onto society through minimum wage increases, tax lamp downs, carbon prices, sugar taxes and a range of other regulations. More and more of companies' un-priced social impacts will become priced financial costs. That process has started but has a lot further to run. As it does, some industries will come under intense pressure and new winners and losers will emerge across the market. Sustain X is the tool we have developed to quantify those impacts in economic terms through rigorous, extensive and detailed analysis and to help us identify future winners and losers. It asks the question if companies were handed a bill for the cost they impose or the benefits they create how large would that credit or debit be. We start by finding as many ways as possible in which companies can affect different stakeholders in society through the products they sell or the way they operate. Activities like paying wages, providing basic utility services or developing new drugs have social benefits that are not listed on financial statements. On the other hand, carbon emissions, alcohol sales or corruption all create un-priced social costs. Nearly all companies have a mix of positive and negative effects. At this point, we've identified around 50 impacts like those. The challenge is to quantify those impacts in economic terms, making them dollar values for individual companies rather than conceptual ideas. We've analysed over 500 academic studies looking at the costs of activities like carbon emissions, alcoholism, water services or medical innovation to create a baseline. We translate those costs to individual companies using the best data we can find. Some from company reports, some from other data providers, and some from more creative use of online and other sources. All of that work gives us a measure of social impact in hard dollar terms which we can use to gauge social impact and the risks to future profitability. We have that analysis for around 9,000 companies giving us a unique view of risks to companies and investments in the future. The implications are profound. Companies face a very different future to the past that lies behind them. Competition, profits and growth rates will be reshaped as costs are redistributed. In total, around half of companies earnings are at risk from the increase in costs they may face. But there are also huge variations. The tobacco, steel and aerospace industries are anchored at the bottom of the table and face huge disruption risks going forward. On the other hand, telecom and healthcare industries are relatively well placed. They bring people access to information and develop the life-saving solutions that should insulate them from some of the pressures ahead. The world is changing. Companies face new pressures and new opportunities. Changes in the social contract between business and society are driving a lot of that change and it's critical that investors prepare. SustainEx helps our analysts, fund managers and clients measure and manage the risks companies and investments face as social impacts become financial costs. It helps us prepare as well as we can for the risks that will emerge in the future, not just those that are already here.
